More Information About the Socioeconomic Peace Index Tool

The Socio-Economic Peacebuilding Index (SEPI) is a composite indicator developed by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) Istanbul International Centre for Private Sector in Development (ICPSD) to measure the structural socio-economic conditions associated with conflict vulnerability in the Horn of Africa, with a focus on Somalia, South Sudan, and Kenya. SEPI is grounded in relative deprivation theory, which states that the gap between perceived entitlements and actual conditions creates grievances that can escalate into collective violence under certain socioeconomic conditions.

SEPI is designed to support development actors, government partners, and donors in identifying where integrated peacebuilding and development investments are most urgently needed in order to develop the conditions for sustained peace.

SEPI is calculated at the Admin-1 (provincial/state) level across Kenya, Somalia, and South Sudan. A high SEPI score reflects how a region has better structural conditions relative to other regions in the same country.

What Does the Map Show?

Each Admin-1 region is shaded on a spectrum from lower capacity (regions with the most deprived socio-economic conditions relative to their country peers) to higher capacity (regions with the strongest relative structural conditions). Five underlying pillars drive the composite score:

Pillar What it captures
Education Index School attendance, literacy, school access, teacher presence
Food Security Index Share of population in IPC Crisis or Worse (Phase 3+)
Poverty Reduction Index Poverty headcount, income/expenditure per capita
Health Access Index Healthcare access, health facility density, hospital density
Climate Resilience Index Vegetation health, soil moisture, drought severity

Hovering over or clicking a region on the map reveals its composite SEPI score, its within-country rank, and its five pillar scores.

Methodology

The Socio-Economic Peacebuilding Index (SEPI) integrates multiple indicators of structural socio-economic capacity to create a comparative assessment tool for peacebuilding programming.

1. Data Collection & Cleaning

  • Household Surveys
  • Satellite Data (NDVI)
  • Conflict Events (ACLED)
  • Health/IPC Data

2. Normalization & Calibration

  • Min-Max Scaling (0–1)
  • Directionality Inversion
  • Weighted geometric mean with equal pillar weights

3. Aggregation

  • Pillar 1: Education
  • Pillar 2: Food Security
  • Pillar 3: Poverty Reduction
  • Pillar 4: Health Access
  • Pillar 5: Climate Resilience

4. SEPI Score

Final Composite Index
(0 – 1 Scale)

Indicator screening: Candidate indicators are reviewed for domain coverage, data quality, and statistical independence before inclusion.

Within-pillar weighting: Each pillar score is the arithmetic mean of its indicators, with equal weight assigned to each sub-indicator.

Validation: SEPI scores are validated against ACLED conflict intensity data and IOM displacement tracking data. Robustness is assessed by re-running the index with alternative normalization methods and weighting schemes.

Visualization: Results are displayed through interactive maps and dashboards enabling users to explore composite scores, pillar breakdowns, and raw indicator values at the Admin-1 level.

Conflict Data

Event counts and fatalities were derived from ACLED data spanning January 2016 to December 2025, incorporating three event types: Battles, Explosions/Remote Violence, and Violence against Civilians.

Battles

A violent interaction between two organized armed groups at a particular time and location. Battles can occur between armed and organized state, non-state, and external groups, and in any combination thereof.

Explosions / remote violence

Incidents in which one side uses weapon types that, by their nature, are at range and widely destructive.

Violence against civilians

Violent events where an organized armed group inflicts violence upon unarmed non-combatants.

What is ACLED?

ACLED (Armed Conflict Location & Event Data) is an independent global monitor that collects, analyses, and maps data on conflict and protest. ACLED provides detailed information to help identify, understand, and track patterns and trends in conflict and crisis situations around the world.

Fatality note: ACLED only records estimated fatalities when reported by the source material. Where possible, researchers triangulate fatality figures, but ACLED does not independently verify them.

FAQ

Why use a geometric mean across pillars instead of a simple average?

The geometric mean ensures that all five dimensions matter and that a high score in one pillar cannot compensate for a low score in another. With a simple arithmetic average, a region that completely fails on food security can still receive a mid-range score if it scores well on health and education. The geometric mean prevents this masking effect — a near-zero score on any single pillar substantially depresses the final score. This reflects the design premise that peacebuilding capacity requires a baseline across all structural dimensions.

How are the sub-indicators weighted?

Each pillar contributes equally to the composite score. Within each pillar, the score is the arithmetic mean of its indicators, meaning each sub-pillar indicator contributes the same fraction (1 / number of indicators) to the pillar score. This is a deliberate transparency choice: equal weighting is reproducible, auditable, and prevents subjective judgments regarding ranking criteria such as “primary school enrollment rate is more important than school accessibility.”

Are scores comparable across Kenya, Somalia, and South Sudan?

No. SEPI scores are min-max normalized within each country, so the reference distribution differs between countries. A score of 0.65 in Kenya and 0.65 in South Sudan reflect different raw underlying conditions.

What years does the data cover?

Data sources span 2019–2026.

  • Food security data (IPC): 2024–2025
  • Health facility data: 2024–2026
  • Education data: 2019–2026
  • Economic indicators: 2022–2024
  • Climate indicators: 2023–2024

See the full sources by clicking each sub-pillar on the map.

Is conflict data included in the SEPI score?

No. Conflict data (ACLED) is used only to validate the index after construction — to test whether lower SEPI scores predict higher conflict intensity and displacement.
